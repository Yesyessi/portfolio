UX/UI Design Mobile Web E-Commerce Sustainability

FlyKind is a mobile-first e-commerce platform for a sustainable fashion brand producing jackets from upcycled parachute canopies.

I designed the limited-batch experience to support the full business system: encouraging customers to buy, making production easier to manage, and giving marketing a clearer story to tell. The result was a more understandable path to purchase built around customization, storytelling, and batch-based manufacturing.

Problem

FlyKind's product was unique by nature, but standard e-commerce patterns could not support the way it needed to be produced, purchased, or understood.

What testing revealed

Users liked the concept, but the experience left too many questions unanswered.

  1. "Batch" was unclear

  2. Buying vs. reserving was confusing

  3. Delivery timing was not obvious

"I like the idea, but I'm not totally sure what happens after I order."

Design challenge

Create a shopping experience that made the product easier to understand, more compelling to buy, and more trustworthy—while also supporting limited inventory, production planning, and a clearer brand story.

Constraints

  1. Non-standard product model — Traditional e-commerce patterns did not fit the product or business needs

  2. User unfamiliarity — Shoppers needed more guidance before committing to purchase

  3. Limited inventory — Each parachute produced only a small number of pieces

  4. Mobile-first context — Most traffic came from social and needed to convert on small screens

  5. High trust needs — Customers needed confidence in quality, fit, timing, and fulfillment

Strategy

To solve this, I introduced a batch model as the core structure behind the experience—helping the site better support how the product was made, sold, and communicated.

Production Alignment

Support limited production and help the business plan demand more effectively.

Solution

Batch System

I introduced a batch system that turned real material limits into a clearer shopping model. Each parachute defined the available capacity for a batch, so the site reflected how the product was actually made rather than standard inventory. As orders came in, capacity reduced in real time, helping customers understand availability while supporting production planning. The system also made the parachute's origin part of the shopping experience, and one piece in each batch received the original parachute tag through a lottery system; adding uniqueness, surprise, and stronger storytelling and marketing value.

How It Works

  1. Material sets capacity — Each parachute determines the total material available, shared across different product types

  2. Mixed product orders — Customers can order different products, and each item uses a portion of the same material capacity

  3. Live batch progress — Orders reduce the remaining material in real time, creating urgency and transparency

  4. Auto-close when full — The batch closes when the material is fully used or time runs out, triggering production

  5. Tag lottery — One piece per batch receives the original parachute tag, adding rarity and storytelling value

Built Trust Through Transparency and Storytelling

  1. Integrated the parachute's origin directly into the shopping experience through batch details, source imagery, and before-to-after context

  2. Made material variations visible so shoppers could understand the uniqueness of each piece, not just the final product

  3. Carried this storytelling across both the custom-made flow and standard product listings to create a clearer and more trustworthy buying experience

Guiding Users Through Checkout

To strengthen purchase confidence, I designed the checkout flow to keep users oriented and informed from step to step. For standard purchases, this meant keeping pricing and actions visible. For the batch flow, it also meant embedding short instructions directly into the sticky bar so users could understand the process without losing momentum.

  1. Sticky total — kept pricing and checkout visible

  2. Sticky instruction — clarified the batch process at key moments

  3. Progress bar — made the flow feel easier to follow

Results

Clarity drove conversion.

Prototype testing with five participants — including two returning FlyKind customers and three new users recruited for the study — showed measurable improvement across every key metric.

28%

Increase in add-to-cart rate in prototype testing vs. original flow

5/5

Participants understood the material origin story without prompting

40%

Reduction in checkout task completion time vs. existing experience

"Now I actually understand what I'm buying. That makes me want to buy it more."

FlyKind reinforced that even though e-commerce has many established best practices, no pattern is truly one size fits all. The brand's one-of-a-kind materials and batch-based model created trust questions that standard shopping flows did not automatically solve, and FlyKind-specific user research made those needs visible early so the experience could be shaped around real expectations instead of assumptions.

It also reminded me why UX/UI matters: it connects a business's unique reality to a customer experience that feels clear, honest, and confident, so when research guides the structure, language, and interactions, the product does not just look better—it becomes easier to trust, easier to use, and more aligned with what the brand stands for.
